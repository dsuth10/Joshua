The Green Gold Crisis

"The shortage drove prices from $3/kg to over $15/kg nationwide. It wasn't just a storm; it was an economic shockwave."

In the pre-dawn stillness of March 20, 2006, the residents of Innisfail heard a sound that few ever forget. It was the roar of 240 kilometre per hour gusts—a shriek described by survivors as a thousand jet engines hovering directly above their rooftops.

Cyclone Larry was a monster of meteorological precision. While Cyclone Tracy (1974) was a compact bullet, Larry was a heavyweight broadside. It struck the Queensland coast as a Category 5 system, the most intense to hit the region in decades.

By the time the eye passed over the town, 10,000 homes had been damaged. In the small community of Silkwood, an astounding 99% of structures were affected. Yet, in a testament to Queensland's evolving safety culture and robust warning systems, the fatality count stood at zero.

“

The bananas weren't just fallen; they were shredded. It looked like someone had run a giant lawnmower over the entire valley.

Local Farmer, 2006

The $1.5 Billion Bill

The true legacy of Larry lay in the red soil of the plantations. Between 80% and 90% of Australia's entire banana crop was wiped out in a single morning. For the next six months, the 'Green Gold' of North Queensland became a luxury item, reaching prices as high as $15 per kilogram.

The economic shock was so severe it added 0.5% to the national inflation rate for the June quarter. It was a stark reminder of how vulnerable modern supply chains remain to the raw power of the elements.

Operation Larry Restore

The recovery effort was a masterclass in civil coordination. The Australian Defence Force was deployed in what was then one of the largest peacetime operations on home soil. General Peter Cosgrove was appointed to lead the recovery taskforce, a move that signalled just how critical the situation was for Northern Queensland's survival.
